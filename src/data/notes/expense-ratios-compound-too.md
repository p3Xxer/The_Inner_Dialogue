Everyone quotes the magic of compounding returns; nobody mentions that fees ride the same curve. A 1% expense ratio doesn't cost 1% — it costs 1% _of an ever-growing base, every year, forever_. Over 30 years at 7% growth, the difference between a 0.1% and a 1% fund is roughly a quarter of the final portfolio. Gone, without a single bad market day.

The mechanism is brutal in its simplicity: the fee is subtracted from the return before it compounds, so you lose the fee _and_ every future return that money would have earned.

Rule I'm keeping: treat the expense ratio as a guaranteed negative return, because that's exactly what it is. Almost nothing a fund promises is guaranteed — this one thing is.
